EECS 376 is Foundations of Computer Science. The public site and syllabus describe it as an introduction to computer science theory with applications.

The course description says it covers design and analysis of algorithms, including divide-and-conquer and dynamic programming. It also covers computability and complexity: how to identify problems that computers cannot solve at all, problems unlikely to be efficiently solvable, and when approximation can be useful.

For course selection, EECS 376 is a theory-heavy class. It is a good fit for students who want deeper algorithmic thinking, proofs, computability, complexity, reductions, approximation, and theoretical limits of computation. It is not mainly a software-building class.

Compared with EECS 281, EECS 376 is more proof and theory focused. Compared with EECS 203, it is more advanced and closer to algorithms, computability, and complexity. It is a useful source for questions about theory requirements, proof-heavy courses, and preparation for advanced CS theory.
